Mean Reversion Strategies

Published on: February 8, 2026

Mean reversion is a systematic trading approach based on the assumption that prices, after deviating significantly from a reference mean, tend to revert to it over time. Unlike trend-following strategies, mean reversion aims to exploit temporary deviations caused by market excesses or short-term inefficiencies.

What Is Mean Reversion

A mean reversion strategy assumes that extreme price movements are statistically unsustainable. The system enters positions when price deviates meaningfully from its historical equilibrium and exits when price returns toward the mean.

Ideal Market Conditions

Mean reverting strategies perform best in range-bound or low-volatility markets, where prices oscillate within well-defined ranges. In the presence of strong trends, these systems may experience significant drawdowns.

Identifying Price Deviations

Statistical and momentum indicators are used to detect overbought and oversold conditions:

  • Bollinger Bands: price touching or breaking the outer bands indicates deviation from the mean.
  • RSI (Relative Strength Index): extreme values signal market excess.
  • Z-Score: measures the distance of price from its mean in standard deviations.

Entry and Exit Rules

Rules must be fully systematic to eliminate discretionary bias:

  • Entry: open long positions in statistically oversold conditions and short positions in overbought conditions.
  • Exit: close positions as price reverts toward the mean or upon reaching a predefined target.

Risk Management

  • Position sizing aligned with market volatility.
  • Hard stop-losses to protect against regime shifts.
  • Overall exposure control across multiple instruments.

Validation and Backtesting

Mean reversion strategies require extensive backtesting, with particular attention to transaction costs and parameter stability. Out-of-sample testing and walk-forward analysis are essential to mitigate overfitting.

Conclusion

A well-designed mean reversion system combines statistical indicators, disciplined execution rules, and conservative risk management. When included in a multi-strategy portfolio, these approaches provide strong diversification benefits relative to trend-following systems.
